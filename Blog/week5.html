Week 5: A Reflection on the Metaphors Used to Understand Digital Information

6 March 2020

I’d never really thought much about the metaphors we use when talking about digital information and the World Wide Web. I’ve just been so used to hearing them that I simply accepted that’s the way things are so I didn’t consider the meaning behind them and why they were used, but the fact that they are used makes so much sense.

The internet and all the technology that has come about because of it are intangible. Describing these concepts using words people are used to and already associate with tangible things makes them easier to fathom. The existing meanings of these words help give meaning to digital technology in a way. They help create a better understanding of things that can be fairly difficult to comprehend, in comparison to our comprehension of actual physical objects such as a desk.

My personal favourite metaphor is ‘cyberspace’. Comparing the internet to space provokes ideas of an endless potential for exploration. The internet is definitely a tool for exploration and gathering information, and the extent of it is about as unimaginable as the extent of space itself, so I think this metaphor is quite fitting. The metaphor of your computer’s ‘desktop’ works well because the desktop on a computer is usually where we keep shortcuts to what we use the most, just like how people keep things that they use often on their physical desks. ‘Cloud’ storage allows us to access data from a computer no matter where we are, just like clouds in the sky are usually seen in any location. One that I hadn’t actually realised to be a metaphor is ‘torrent’. Torrent refers to a strong stream of water, as well as a way of downloading files in parts from multiple computers. The comparison made gives us the idea that torrenting files is fast, like a strong stream of water.
